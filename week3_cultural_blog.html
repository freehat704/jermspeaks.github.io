Reponse to the Fireside Chat

Date: May 6th, 2014

The DBC experience seems very rooted in its core beliefs of engineering empathy, which I believe distinguishes it from many other programs. After watching the video, I found myself agreeing with Shereef about the kitchen and the restaurant metaphor because I flipped my own views on travel in about the same way.

When I was growing up, my family went on a bunch of vacations and always bought tour package deals with the intent as one goes to a restaurant: you only go for the service and expect to get certain accomadations from it and nothing more. When I finally traveled on my own in my late teens, I discovered a world of exploration and wonder since I wasn't following a tour guide or sleeping in hotels. I met locals instead of meeting other tourists. I ate dinner at the marketplaces instead of at the continental restaurants. It was exhilarating and exciting, especially since I gave myself up to possibility rather than accomadations. Then, I wondered how come no one else travels this way.

With my education background, I was a little of both the restaurant goer and the line cooks at the kitchen. For some classes, I just wanted the grade because I didn't care about the general ed subjects. For my college upper division courses, I felt cheated every time our lectures didn't go on for hours on a subject that mattered to me. For college project classes, I felt like the full reign and open subjects allowed me to thrive and really take control of what I was learning. But it was all abrupt and I wanted the aspects of learning without the aspects of taking classes that would only amount to less content than I intended. It probably also helped that I spent a considerable amount of my free time in a machine shop because that was also my playground.

That's what I want from DBC, a playground of tools and a good chunk of time just messing around with it all to make my projects the way I want them to. The big difference between these two is the feedback time. Physical objects, or hardware in general, has a longer cycle time in product development and manufacturing. With programming, especially with proper version control, you can iterate at a much faster pace.

DBC embodies the culture of how to make yourself a whole person rather than pumping out degrees, as colleges do. I was reading a while back about stoics and how some of them used to intentionally put themselves in unconfortable situations so that when the time comes to face a similar situation, they can deal with it a lot easier. One of the things in the video that struck a cord with me is how we are allowed to be a beginner. I'm an avid reader of zen, not so good at practicing, and what Shunryu Suzuki says about beginner's mind embodies the point Shereef was making with only knowing so much, and having the attitude to try to learn more. Suzuki says that a beginner's mind plays around with meditation and tries new things to reach that point where you don't think anymore, that you've been removed from yourself. A master's mind finds themselves following the same path they've used before, and can become arrogrant with trying to remove themselves during meditation.

I'm hoping in putting all of my effort in engaging myself in this culture. I don't care if someone else thinks this as the rotten Koolaid because I'll be able to take the feedback a lot better and counter with that person with better responses, hopefully without emotions tied to it. It's really important in the long run anyways because we'll always be faced with similar situations where we don't understand something and need someone to explain it to us or have the ability to figure it out for ourselves. My expectations haven't changed about dbc. In fact, they are more heigtened and aware that this atmosphere really is what I want it to be, and more. It makes me incredibly excited.
